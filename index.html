Concept & Technical Brief — Circles

Circles isn't a contacts list. It's the instrument panel for how you actually work a network.

What it's for, how it's visually encoded, and how the control layer gives someone real leverage over their own graph rather than a fixed view of it.

01 — The reframing

Job search, outreach, and engagement are usually three separate apps. They're one relationship.

Most products that touch "your network" pick one lens and ignore the other two. A CRM tracks outreach but has no idea what you've actually shipped with someone. A contacts list shows who you know but nothing about temperature — it can't tell a person you talk to weekly from one you emailed cold eight months ago and never heard back from.

Circles treats those as the same underlying graph, viewed one way. A cold recruiter outreach, a warm collaborator from a shipped project, and a person whose posts you keep engaging with are all just nodes with different edge properties — not three separate systems that happen to share a UI.

02 — Why a node graph

The rest of this site already breaks from rigid page structure. Circles takes that furthest.

Home and Feeds are both, underneath their Mondrian framing, infinite pannable canvases — not paginated lists pretending to be something more dynamic. Circles pushes that same instinct all the way: a force-directed graph, full-bleed, with the bento framing shrunk down to a thin instrument panel around it rather than the dominant structure.

The panel doesn't disappear — the grid lines, hairline rules, and mono labels that define this site's visual language are still there, framing the graph the way a cockpit frames a windscreen. But the graph itself is physics, not HTML flow.

03 — What a node is

Shape and border carry real information, not decoration.

Every node encodes two independent facts at a glance: what kind of relationship this is (shape) and how close it sits to you (border). Nothing relies on color — the whole site stays strictly monochrome, so archetype had to be legible in silhouette alone.

Archetype → shape
PeerBuilt something together, or shares a room
Hiring managerOn the deciding side of a role
RecruiterAn intermediary — routes you elsewhere
MentorAdvisory — no open loop to close
Proximity → border
Inner circleSolid border, first-degree
Outer circleDashed border, second-degree or early-stage

Fill follows the same logic — inner and "you" render solid, outer renders hollow, so density near center is legible before reading a label.

04 — What an edge is

The responsiveness spectrum is the part that replaces a CRM.

Every connection sits on a six-point scale, and the line itself communicates it. Warmer relationships pull harder in the physics — a real spring-strength difference, not just a visual one.

WarmSolid, full opacity
ConsideredSolid, slightly faded
CasualDashed, occasional contact
LightDashed, faint
ColdDotted + arrow — sent, no reply
GhostedSame, further faded

The arrow on cold and ghosted edges matters: those aren't mutual relationships rendered dimly, they're outreach that hasn't been reciprocated. If someone replies, the edge resolves — dashed and directional becomes solid and undirected. The graph doesn't just display the pipeline, it is the pipeline.

05 — Agency, not filters

Every control changes the simulation itself, not just what's dimmed.

The instinct with most "network" tools is to bury the ranking logic behind a search bar. Circles does the opposite: the controls are the algorithm, exposed directly.

Range

Sets which degree of connection is even loaded, not just shown. Narrowing to "1st" removes second-degree nodes from the simulation entirely.

Inner ⟷ Outer bias

A literal radial force added to the physics each frame — a camera control over the simulation, not a toggle over a static image.

Archetype

Isolate peers, hiring managers, recruiters, or mentors — "who do I know that could vouch for me at a company that's hiring."

Responsiveness

Filter straight to the cold or ghosted end. This is the control that turns a pretty graph into an actual to-do list.

06 — How it's actually built

Hand-rolled physics on canvas — not a graph library bolted on.

This site already leans on canvas for anything that needs to feel physical rather than templated — the particle-field hero, the infinite pannable card grids. Circles follows the same discipline.

01
Force simulation

Pairwise repulsion, spring attraction along each edge (strength set by responsiveness), a gentle pull to center, and a user-controlled radial bias on top.

02
Canvas rendering, not DOM nodes

Redraws every frame at 2D canvas speed — the only way this stays smooth while panning, zooming, and dragging at once.

03
One anchor point

The "you" node is the single fixed point. Drag any other node and it pins under the cursor, then releases back into the physics on let-go.

04
Shared interaction language

Clicking any node opens the same poster panel used for every other card on the site — a new way to browse the network, not a separate product.

07 — Scope, honestly

What this is now, and what it deliberately isn't yet.

In scope for this pass
  • Full node/edge visual system, all six responsiveness states
  • Real physics-backed range, bias, archetype, and responsiveness controls
  • Pan, zoom, node drag, click-to-open on every node
  • Populated with representative people, not empty scaffolding
Deliberately deferred
  • The 3D globe as a geographic counterpart view — a toggle between "relationship space" and "map space"
  • Real connection counts — demo data doesn't match the "128 connections" figure shown elsewhere yet
  • Automatic responsiveness scoring from real interaction history

Concept & Technical Brief — General Assembly

This isn’t a skills page dressed up as a gallery. It’s the one place on this site built to be looked at, not read.

What it’s for, why it breaks the site’s monochrome rule on purpose, and how the interaction here is deliberately different from every other card on the site.

01 — The reframing

A repertoire of buzzwords turned into a photo wall would have been the safe version of this page.

General Assembly could have stayed a grid of skill-tags and case-study fragments — useful, but easy to skim past without feeling anything. Recasting it as a photo wall does something a tag cloud can’t: it makes the page worth lingering on for its own sake, not just for what it proves.

The images aren’t illustrations of a skill list sitting somewhere else. They’re the actual raw material here — a genuine change in what this page is for, not a reskin of what it looked like before.

02 — Why an infinite canvas

The same pannable, tiled surface as Home and Feeds — the site’s native format, not a special case.

Every major browsing surface on this site already breaks from a scrolling list in favor of an infinite, pannable grid. General Assembly doesn’t introduce a new interaction model — it just points that same camera at photography instead of text.

That consistency matters more than it looks: someone who’s already learned to drag-pan Feeds or Home doesn’t have to relearn anything to use this page.

03 — What a tile is

Full color, full bleed — the one deliberate exception on a monochrome site.

Every other surface here stays strictly black, white, and gray, including photography used elsewhere on the site. This page is the single place that breaks that discipline on purpose.

No grayscale filter, no desaturation. Each tile is a real photograph, cropped to fill its cell edge-to-edge, with a small index number as its only chrome. The restraint everywhere else is exactly what makes the color here read as a decision, not an oversight.

04 — What happens on interaction

Hover lifts a shadow. Click opens an invitation, not a data sheet.

Every other clickable card on this site opens the same wide poster panel — kicker, headline, tag row, related pathways. This page deliberately doesn’t. Hovering a tile just deepens its shadow and lifts it slightly, a pure elevation cue with nowhere to click through to yet.

Clicking opens a small, centered card sized closer to an actual postcard than a sidebar — the photo up top, a caption set in italic serif underneath, nothing else competing for attention. It’s meant to feel like being handed something, not like opening a record.

05 — Real controls, not decoration

Grid size and image gap both touch the actual layout math, not a filter.

Two sliders live in the bottom drawer: grid size, which changes the tile dimensions used by the same panning physics as every other canvas grid on the site, and image gap, deliberately built as an independent control rather than tied to that same scale — so density and size can be tuned separately.

Neither is cosmetic. Move either one and the whole tiled pattern recomputes and re-centers live.

06 — How it’s actually built

One shared grid engine, extended with two small hooks rather than forked.

This page runs on the exact same panning, zoom, and tiling engine as Home and Feeds — not a parallel implementation that happens to look similar.

01
Shared canvas-grid engine

Two small opt-in hooks were added to support this page — a custom tile renderer for full-bleed images, and a custom click handler — both inert unless a page explicitly asks for them.

02
Deduplicated source set

The photo pool was pulled from a longer list that included the same images uploaded twice in different formats. Each appears once.

03
The postcard is a separate object

Rather than resizing the site’s shared poster panel down to postcard scale, this page opens an entirely separate, smaller overlay — so the panel used everywhere else stays exactly as it is.

04
Independent margin control

Image gap required a small addition to the shared grid engine itself — previously, spacing was locked to the same scale as tile size. It’s now a separate multiplier any grid can opt into.

07 — Scope, honestly

What this is now, and what it deliberately isn’t yet.

In scope for this pass
  • Full-color infinite canvas populated with real photography, not placeholders
  • Independent grid-size and image-gap controls
  • A distinct postcard-style interaction, separate from the site’s shared panel
  • Deduplicated from the original source list — one image per photo
Deliberately deferred
  • The social-wall version — aggregating contributions from people in your Circles, weighted by relationship preference, not just your own photography
  • Per-photo captions and context, rather than a small rotating set of placeholder lines
  • Contributor attribution, once this becomes genuinely multi-person
